Introduction¶

For this project I have chosen to investigate the excerpted data from the FBI's National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS) along with the supplemental Census population data by state. Federal law requires that Federal Firearm License (FFL) sellers to run background checks on prospective buyers through the NICS after it became operational in 1998. The NICS provides background check data for the purchase/transfer of both handguns and long guns (rifles) to eligible persons. Per Federal law, the FBI has 3 business days to allow or deny the transfer; after which the FFL may legally proceed with the transfer.

NICS Notes: via Wikipedia, fbi.gov

  • "Most checks are determined within minutes. If a determination is not obtained within three business days then the transfer may legally be completed."
  • "Background checks are not required under federal law for intrastate firearm transfers between private parties."
  • "States may implement their own NICS programs. Such states become the point of contact (POC) between their FFL dealers and the NICS. A few partial-POC states run FFL handgun checks, while the FBI runs long gun checks."
  • "NICS is located at the FBIs Criminal Justice Information Services Division in Clarksburg, West Virginia. It provides full service to FFLs in 30 states, five U.S. territories, and the District of Columbia" does this jive with data? looks like all 50 states are included in NICS gun_data

    Gun rights, control and regulation are a collection of extremely divise topics in the United States today. The implementation of Federal legislation, such as the requirement for background checks and the NICS system of databases, are often at odds with state regulations that either seek to ease restrictions around gun ownership or provide for better enforcement of existing regulations. For this and other reasons there exists a broad spectrum of policies at the state level that contribute to the politics at play. In this exploratory data analysis I will examine data from a subset of the larger United States. These states come from either end of the gun control debate with laws and regulations that seek to either ensure a strict interpretation of the US Constitution's 2nd Amendment or provide for a modern regulatory framework that limits the ownership and transfer of firearms.

Conclusions¶

In conclusion we have become aware, by examining the FBI NICS and census data across the states selected for our analysis, that there are dinstinct population characteristics between those states with conservative gun rights lawsm and those with liberal gun control laws. This does not by any means imply causation! Nor does it necessarily demonstrate any correlation between the population traits chosen for this analysis -- proportion of population with a college degree and proportion of the population living below the Federal povery line -- but we may observe that these states do indeed display stark differences in these particular variables.

Some of the limitations included a lack of accurate date information for the census data. For instance, we do not know for which year the percent in poverty or percent with college degree are for. Nor do we have any historical data to identify changes or trends over time. Also, the census data often provides ranges of years (2011 - 2015) that made them difficult to interpret ie. are these mean values for this time period? To further improve this analysis, and given more time to do so, it would be prudent to seek out better updated census data from the census.gov website.
